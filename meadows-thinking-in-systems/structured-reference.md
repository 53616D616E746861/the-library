## Glossary

**Archetypes:** Common system structures that produce characteristic patterns of behavior.
**Balancing feedback loop:** A stabilizing, goal-seeking loop that opposes or reverses change imposed on the system.
**Bounded rationality:** Logic that makes sense within one part of the system but is not reasonable in the broader context.
**Dynamic equilibrium:** Stock level is steady despite ongoing inflows and outflows (inflows = outflows).
**Feedback loop:** A closed chain of causal connections from a stock, through decisions/rules dependent on the stock level, back through a flow to change the stock.
**Flow:** Material or information that enters or leaves a stock over time.
**Hierarchy:** Subsystems within systems, organized to create a larger system.
**Limiting factor:** The system input that is currently constraining activity.
**Nonlinear relationship:** Cause does not produce a proportional effect.
**Reinforcing feedback loop:** An amplifying loop that reinforces the direction of change (vicious or virtuous cycles).
**Resilience:** The ability to recover from perturbation.
**Self-organization:** The ability of a system to create new structure, learn, or diversify.
**Shifting dominance:** Change over time in the relative strengths of competing feedback loops.
**Stock:** An accumulation of material or information that has built up over time.
**Suboptimization:** A subsystem's goals dominating at the expense of the total system's goals.
**System:** A set of elements coherently organized and interconnected in a pattern that produces a characteristic set of behaviors, often classified as its "function" or "purpose."

## Summary of Systems Principles

### Systems
- A system is more than the sum of its parts.
- Many interconnections operate through the flow of information.
- The least obvious part — function or purpose — is often the most crucial determinant of behavior.
- System structure is the source of behavior. Behavior reveals itself as a series of events over time.

### Stocks, Flows, and Dynamic Equilibrium
- A stock is the memory of changing flows within the system.
- Inflows > outflows: stock rises. Outflows > inflows: stock falls. Equal: dynamic equilibrium.
- A stock can be increased by decreasing outflow as well as increasing inflow.
- Stocks act as delays, buffers, or shock absorbers.
- Stocks allow inflows and outflows to be decoupled and independent.

### Feedback Loops
- Balancing loops are equilibrating/goal-seeking — sources of stability and resistance to change.
- Reinforcing loops are self-enhancing — leading to exponential growth or collapse.
- Feedback can only affect future behavior; it cannot correct the behavior that drove the current feedback.
- A balancing loop must have its goal set appropriately to compensate for all draining/inflowing processes, or the process will miss its target.
- Systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors.

### Shifting Dominance, Delays, and Oscillations
- Complex behaviors arise as relative strengths of feedback loops shift.
- A delay in a balancing feedback loop makes the system likely to oscillate.
- Changing the length of a delay may make a large change in behavior.

### Constraints on Systems
- Exponentially growing systems always have at least one reinforcing loop AND one balancing loop.
- No system can grow forever in a finite environment.
- Nonrenewable resources are stock-limited. Renewable resources are flow-limited.

### Resilience, Self-Organization, and Hierarchy
- There are always limits to resilience.
- Systems need to be managed for resilience, not only productivity or stability.
- Systems often have the ability to self-organize — to create new structure, learn, diversify, complexify.
- Hierarchical systems evolve from the bottom up. Upper layers serve the purposes of lower layers.

### Sources of System Surprises
- Many relationships are nonlinear.
- There are no separate systems — the world is a continuum. Boundaries depend on the purpose of the discussion.
- At any given time, the most important input is the one that is most limiting.
- There always will be limits to growth.
- A quantity growing exponentially toward a limit reaches that limit in a surprisingly short time.
- When there are long delays in feedback loops, foresight is essential.
- Bounded rationality of individual actors may not further the welfare of the system as a whole.

### Mindsets and Models
- Everything we think we know about the world is a model.
- Our models do have a strong congruence with the world.
- Our models fall far short of representing the real world fully.

## System Traps (and Ways Out)

### 1. Policy Resistance
**Trap:** Multiple actors pull a system toward different goals. Any effective new policy pulls the state away from others' goals, producing resistance. Everyone expends effort maintaining a result no one wants.
**Way Out:** Let go. Bring in all actors. Seek mutually satisfactory goals — or redefine toward larger shared goals.

### 2. Tragedy of the Commons
**Trap:** Shared resource — each user benefits from use but shares costs of abuse. Weak feedback from resource condition to users. Consequence: overuse until unavailable.
**Way Out:** Educate users. Restore/strengthen the missing feedback link (privatize so each user feels direct consequences, or regulate access).

### 3. Drift to Low Performance
**Trap:** Performance standards influenced by past performance, especially with negative bias. Sets up a reinforcing loop of eroding goals → drifting toward low performance.
**Way Out:** Keep standards absolute. Let standards be enhanced by best actual performances, not degraded by worst.

### 4. Escalation
**Trap:** Competitive reinforcing loop — each side tries to surpass the other. Exponential escalation to extremes.
**Way Out:** Refuse to compete (unilaterally disarm), interrupting the reinforcing loop. Or negotiate balancing loops to control escalation.

### 5. Success to the Successful
**Trap:** Winners rewarded with means to win again. Reinforcing loop: winners take all, losers are eliminated.
**Way Out:** Diversification; limits on any one winner's share; policies that level the field; rewards that don't bias the next round.

### 6. Shifting the Burden to the Intervenor
**Trap:** A solution reduces symptoms but not the underlying problem. If the intervention erodes the system's self-maintaining capacity, a destructive reinforcing loop begins: system deteriorates → needs more intervention → deteriorates further.
**Way Out:** Avoid getting in. If already in: restore the system's own capacity, then remove yourself. The longer you wait, the harder withdrawal becomes.

### 7. Rule Beating
**Trap:** Rules produce perverse behavior — meeting the letter while distorting the system.
**Way Out:** Redesign rules to release creativity toward achieving the purpose of the rules, not toward beating them.

### 8. Seeking the Wrong Goal
**Trap:** Inaccurate or incomplete goal indicators. System obediently produces what the indicators reward, which isn't what was actually wanted.
**Way Out:** Specify indicators reflecting real welfare. Don't confuse effort with result — a system optimizing for effort produces effort, not outcomes.

## Guidelines for Living in a World of Systems

1. Get the beat of the system.
2. Expose your mental models to the light of day.
3. Honor, respect, and distribute information.
4. Use language with care and enrich it with systems concepts.
5. Pay attention to what is important, not just what is quantifiable.
6. Make feedback policies for feedback systems.
7. Go for the good of the whole.
8. Listen to the wisdom of the system.
9. Locate responsibility within the system.
10. Stay humble — stay a learner.
11. Celebrate complexity.
12. Expand time horizons.
13. Defy the disciplines.
14. Expand the boundary of caring.
15. Don't erode the goal of goodness.
