# Richard Feynman: The Difference Between Knowing the Name of Something and Knowing Something
Source: https://fs.blog/richard-feynman-knowing-something/
Fetched: 2026-04-27

Richard Feynman (1918-1988) was an extraordinary thinker who believed "the world is much more interesting than any one discipline." His explanations across topics -- from scientific inquiry to everyday phenomena -- demonstrated remarkable clarity and insight.

## The Name vs. Understanding Distinction

Feynman illustrated this crucial difference through the example of a bird:

> See that bird? It's a brown-throated thrush, but in Germany it's called a halzenfugel... even if you know all those names for it, you still know nothing about the bird.

Knowing terminology doesn't equate to genuine comprehension. We often mask our lack of understanding with vague, fact-deficient language.

## How to Actually Learn

Rather than memorizing definitions, Feynman advocated for deeper engagement. He critiqued first-grade science books that taught "energy makes it move" without explaining the underlying mechanisms. The real learning comes from decomposing systems to see their components and design.

The proper approach, he insisted, is to "Take apart the toy; see how it works." This hands-on investigation builds genuine understanding rather than hollow vocabulary.

## Testing Your Understanding

Feynman proposed a practical test: explain what you've learned without using the newly learned word. If you cannot rephrase the concept independently, you've only memorized definitions, not internalized knowledge.
