## About

An accessible primer on Popper's falsifiability criterion — the assertion that for any hypothesis to have scientific credence, it must be inherently disprovable. Covers the distinction between falsifiability in principle and falsifiability in practice, the relationship to pseudoscience, and the major limitation: falsifiability is strict and does not account for observational and descriptive sciences. Uses Newton/Einstein as a worked example of how falsifiable theories are refined rather than discarded wholesale.

**Entity:** falsifiability
**Type:** concept

**Summary:** Popper's demarcation criterion: a hypothesis must be inherently disprovable to qualify as scientific. Falsifiability says nothing about correctness — only about whether a claim can be engaged with scientifically. The dividing line between what is science and what isn't. Limitation: strict application excludes observational sciences (anthropology, sociology) that advance knowledge through case studies rather than falsifiable predictions.
