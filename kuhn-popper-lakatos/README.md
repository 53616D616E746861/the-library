## About

A concise comparison of three major positions in the philosophy of science: Popper's falsificationism (theories rejected at first conflict with observation), Kuhn's paradigm shifts (normal science within paradigms, irrational shifts between them), and Lakatos's research programmes (a middle path where theories have a hard core shielded by a protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses, and the question shifts from true/false to progressive/degenerating).

Lakatos is more useful for agent work than Popper because research programmes can be temporarily degenerating without being abandoned — the same way an agent's working framework can hit anomalies without requiring wholesale replacement.

**Summary:** Three positions on how science progresses. Popper: falsification at first conflict. Kuhn: normal science within paradigms, irrational shifts between them. Lakatos: research programmes with hard core and protective belt — the question is whether a programme is progressive (novel predictions verified) or degenerating (no growth, auxiliary belt doesn't produce novel predictions). Lakatos avoids Popper's problem of premature rejection and Kuhn's incommensurability.

**Key concepts:**

| Concept | Type | Summary |
|---------|------|---------|
| Research programme | framework | Lakatos: a "theory" is really a succession of slightly different theories sharing a common hard core, shielded by a protective belt of auxiliary hypotheses. Progressive programmes predict novel facts; degenerating ones don't. |
| Protective belt | concept | Auxiliary hypotheses that shield the hard core from falsification. Adjusting the belt is normal science, not ad hoc rescue — as long as the programme remains progressive. |
| Progressive vs degenerating | distinction | The replacement for true/false. A programme is progressive if its modifications lead to novel predictions that are later verified. Degenerating if modifications are only defensive and predict nothing new. |
